MedTok

The Anatomy of Medical Image Tokenization

Benchmark Medical Imaging NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks
Code Paper (arXiv) MedMNIST

Abstract

Medical image generation and analysis increasingly rely on tokenization—mapping images to compact latent representations. Recent work on natural images has revealed fundamental tensions: reconstruction FID (rFID) is often poorly correlated with generation FID (gFID); increasing latent dimension improves reconstruction but hurts generation; alignment strategies yield mixed results. Yet all such findings stem from ImageNet-scale natural imagery. The design space of tokenizers for medical imaging remains underexplored, with no systematic benchmark to guide practitioners. We present MedTok, a comprehensive benchmark evaluating 15+ tokenizers across 12 MedMNIST datasets, spanning continuous VAEs, discrete quantizers, and alignment strategies. Our key findings: (1) In medical imaging, rFID correlates strongly with gFID (Pearson r ≈ 0.79); (2) RQVAE, SimQinco, and SimVQ dominate discrete tokenizers; (3) alignment improves reconstruction but degrades generation; (4) larger latent dimensions hurt generation.

Key Findings

rFID predicts generation. Unlike natural images, rFID correlates strongly with gFID (r ≈ 0.79) in medical imaging.
RQVAE, SimQinco, SimVQ lead discrete tokenizers in reconstruction and compression efficiency.
Alignment (MedVAE, VAVAE) improves reconstruction but neither improves generation; VAEKL remains a strong baseline.
Larger latent dimensions degrade generation; use moderate d (5–15) for generation-oriented pipelines.
